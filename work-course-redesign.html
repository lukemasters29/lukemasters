Portfolio Course Modernization Project Experience
Case Study · UX & Instructional Design

Information Systems Course Redesign: From Passive Lecture to Experiential Learning

Client Dr. Kathleen Hartzel · Duquesne University
Role Instructional Designer · Guest Lecturer
Timeline Aug 2025 – Present
Deliverables Lecture Deck · Assignment · Live Workshop
The Problem

Students weren't disengaged. The experience was.

After taking Dr. Hartzel's Information Systems course, I found myself walking away with terminology but not understanding. The class relied heavily on passive lecture and abstract vocabulary — students were asked to memorize concepts before ever seeing why they mattered. The result was predictable: disengagement, surface-level retention, and a widening gap between academic content and real-world practice.

I approached Dr. Hartzel with a proposal: let me redesign the module on automation. Not just the slides — the entire learning experience.

"The curriculum was vocabulary-first. Students were handed a dictionary before they'd ever seen the language spoken."

The core friction · Identified through personal experience as a student
The Process

A collaboration built over multiple working sessions.

This wasn't a one-time deliverable. Dr. Hartzel and I worked across several meetings — planning the scope, stress-testing assignment ideas, building and demoing a live automation workflow together, and walking her through Power Automate so she could confidently support students going forward.

01
Discovery & Scope Planning

Met with Dr. Hartzel to identify the specific pain points in the existing module. Agreed on the redesign scope: a new lecture deck on process automation and a hands-on assignment students would build, not just read about.

02
Visual & Pedagogical Redesign of the Lecture

Overhauled the PowerPoint presentation from text-heavy slides to concept-driven visual explanations. The new deck covers automation fundamentals, Power Automate architecture, AI-in-automation, and a full guided tour of the platform — 24 slides built for first-time learners.

03
Assignment Design: Application-First Learning

Created a scenario-based assignment where students step into the role of a product analyst intern tasked with solving a real business problem. Rather than a presentation or a quiz, students build a working 3-node Power Automate workflow from scratch.

04
Teaching Dr. Hartzel Power Automate

To ensure the module could live beyond my involvement, I walked Dr. Hartzel through the platform — triggers, actions, run history, debugging failed flows — so she could confidently guide future students independently.

05
Live Teaching & Workshop Delivery

Stood in front of Dr. Hartzel's class and taught the session myself — presenting the redesigned deck, then walking students through the assignment demo live. Students followed along in real time, actively building alongside the lecture.

Deliverables

Two artifacts. One shift in how the course works.

Deliverable 01 · Lecture Deck — 24 Slides
Modern Process Automation for Systems Analysis & Design

A complete visual redesign of the automation module — moving away from text-dense slides toward concept-first explanations. The deck covers why businesses automate, the four core building blocks of any automation (triggers, actions, conditions, loops), where AI fits into modern workflows, and a full guided tour of Microsoft Power Automate's interface. Each concept is grounded in a real-world business scenario students can immediately recognize.

Deliverable 02 · Assignment — Hands-On Build
The "Carrie" Scenario: Build a Real Automation Workflow

Instead of a presentation or sketch, students are handed a business scenario and asked to solve it. The assignment casts them as a product analyst intern at Americas First Bank, tasked with replacing a manual, email-based website change request process with an automated Power Automate workflow. The design constraint is intentional: build it in only three nodes.

Assignment Scenario Americas First Bank · Website Change Request Workflow

The user: Carrie is a project manager at Americas First Bank. She manages all website change requests from internal departments — but currently does it entirely through email. Requests come in inconsistent formats, get buried in her inbox, and she routinely misses or delays responses.

The task: Students are hired as product analyst interns to replace Carrie's manual process with an automated workflow. They must build a Microsoft Form to capture structured request data, then wire it to a Power Automate flow that triggers on form submission, retrieves the response, and sends Carrie a clean, consistently formatted email — all in three nodes.

Why it works: The scenario is grounded in a real problem class of inefficiency every organization faces. Students aren't memorizing a definition of "automation" — they're building a solution for a person with a name, a job, and a frustration they can empathize with.

The Impact

Dr. Hartzel adopted the materials. The class followed along.

The redesigned module was integrated directly into Dr. Hartzel's curriculum. On the day of the lecture, students didn't just watch — they built alongside the demo in real time, with visible engagement throughout the session. The application-first approach did what passive lecture couldn't: it made the material stick by making it immediately applicable.

Beyond the classroom, this engagement led to a continuing role — working with Dr. Hartzel on future course development and returning as a guest lecturer as the curriculum evolves.

Reflection

What this project is really about.

A course curriculum is a product. Students are users. Disengagement is a friction point — a signal that the experience isn't meeting the user where they are. This project is a demonstration of the same instinct that drives good UX: identify where the experience breaks down, understand why, and redesign it around how people actually learn.

The tools happened to be PowerPoint and Power Automate. The discipline was experience design.
